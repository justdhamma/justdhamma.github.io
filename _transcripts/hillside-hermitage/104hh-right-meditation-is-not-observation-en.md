Critique of a Meditation Technique

Bhikkhu: Many years ago I was taught the S. N. Goenka’s Vipassanā method. It

emphasized focusing on bodily sensations and claimed that this meant attending

only to what is “purely physical,” without imagery, interpretation, or any mental

overlay. You were instructed to access the bare physical pressure or whatever

sensation appeared in the body. That was the first step. The second was to avoid any

mental reaction to those sensations, because the method identified such reactions as

what the Buddha called saṅkhāra.

Ajahn Nyanamoli: Isn't that already your reaction?

B: Exactly. But the idea is that you don't react to those physical sensations. If you

don't react, then you're not creating new sankhāras. And when you're not creating

new sankhāras, then old sankhāras come to the surface as physical sensations that,

again, you don't react to. If you keep not reacting to this process, then you're

removing, getting rid of, or purifying an old stock of reactions, old sankhāras.

Nm: How would you know that?

B: Because in the future, if you do it for long enough, you'll become enlightened;

you'll get rid of all your past stock of sankhāras. You can't know that now, but if you

keep doing this, at some point in the future...

Nm: So just take it on trust, without being able to verify it now.

## B: Yes.

The Problem of “Past” Sankhāras

Nm: The idea that present physical sensations are manifestations of past sankhāras,

which must be diminished by focusing on them without reaction, is not only an

assumption, since there is no way to verify that accumulated sankhāras mysteriously

manifest as bodily sensations, but also a contradiction. That practice emphasizes

nonreaction, yet the entire practice itself is a reaction to those very sensations. It

assumes unverifiable causes and is practically inconsistent with its own principle of

nonreaction.



## 2

B: Exactly. But it provides a story.

Nm: It offers a comfortable psychological explanation which requires no existential

effort that might provoke anxiety or doubt. All one has to do is observe these random

sensations, while enjoying the fanciful, reassuring story of energetic purification.

B: That's it. It provides you with a motivation to keep doing this technique with the

faith that in the future I might have some peak experience.

Nm: You have to start by taking things on faith, but the things you want to take on

faith are the things you want to then be able to verify and thus replace that faith with

confidence, as the suttas say. But this you can't verify. How can you become

conscious of these unconscious sankhāras before they manifest and then know them

as being there, waiting to show themselves on the surface of your body? It's just

assumption on top of assumption that you cannot verify; you're just acting out of it

because it pleases you.

B: That's one fundamental problem. And also, how can you experience pure

physicality?

Nm: Yes, that was the first point you raised - attending to something purely physical,

having no thought of it in your mind. However, if it's purely physical, it means you

aren't thinking about it, yet how are you talking about it? How are you knowing it?

How can you attend to it? Attention is not physical. Anything that appears is related

to the mind

B: That's where things appear.

Nm: That's why everything is a dhamma, and why dhammas are the objects of the

faculty of the mind. Yet here you are, practicing Buddhism, practicing the Dhamma,

rooted in a belief that you can perceive matter non-mentally - that there's a pure

experience of matter with no thought or mind involved. How is that even

conceivable, except as a thought?

B: Exactly. So that's another problem. Another problem is, how can you choose to

not react?

Nm: Yes, that was the second point you raised. So, there are these sensations

happening in my body, and I am choosing to not react to them.

B: Which is your current reaction.

Nm: Exactly. It might be a better reaction than reacting impulsively, but restraining

yourself is still a reaction. So if you're developing a belief that your restraining of a

particular type of reaction - on account of the action of restraint - is not a reaction,

that's a wrong view because it contradicts itself.



## 3

Yes, you should restrain yourself if that's what you're doing. But if you're restraining

yourself while holding the view that restraining yourself is not an action, not a

choice, but something completely beyond it, it means you will not look for that which

truly is beyond it. That's why sense-restraint itself cannot result in enlightenment.

It's a good thing to do, but it cannot enlighten you because, as you can see, you can

still hold and develop even further wrong views on account of it.

The Nature of Sankhāras

B: That teaching claims you attend to “pure matter” by feeling it, yet the descriptions

of that feeling are pressure, throbbing, and tingling. These are perceptions, so feeling

and perception end up collapsed into one category. It also states that saṅkhāra

appears as matter because past intentions supposedly manifest as the material you

now feel. Everything becomes blended together, and it is unclear how continuing

with that framework could clarify the distinct functions of the aggregates.

Nm: Also, if this idea of “my past saṅkhāras” cannot be directly verified, how could

anyone have discovered it in the first place? The first person to awaken could not

have known such a doctrine in advance, nor could he have accepted it on trust

because no one was there to teach it. So how did the Buddha become the Buddha? By

examining what can actually be verified and then instructing others to verify the

same.

And if I say to you, This is your unconscious process, that already contradicts itself.

If it is unconscious, you are not aware of it, which means you cannot meaningfully

designate or describe it. The moment you can talk about it, you are conscious of it,

and it is no longer something unconscious.

B: I guess the question arises then: if sankhāras aren't this past stock of your

previous reactions that manifest on your body physically...If that's the wrong way of

thinking about sankhāra, then what did the Buddha mean when he talked about it?

Nm: Most people, including teachers, do not examine this closely. They offer quick

explanations or use the term to cover anything unclear that someone reports:

energies, theories, thinking patterns. Everything becomes “sankhāra, sankhāra.” It

turns into a vague mystical force that must be overcome. But the Pāli term saṅkhāra

literally means putting together, holding together, or constructing. It refers to the

activity that determines the sense of self.

You want to see saṅkhāra from the standpoint of self-view and appropriation. Once

you see it there, the saṅkhāras disappear. So what is it for someone who still has a

sense of self, which always involves ownership? Now compare that to someone who

no longer maintains any ownership. The difference is not in the things themselves.

They remain exactly as they were. The difference lies in the presence or absence of

appropriation. The presence of saṅkhāra is the presence of taking things as “mine.”

The absence of saṅkhāra is the absence of taking things as “mine.”



## 4

The act of taking things as “mine” constructs and sustains the sense of self. That is

why saṅkhāras are called formations or determinations or the makers. Without

appropriation, there is no activity that builds or sustains the sense of self. Stop taking

things as “mine,” and the sense of self loses its support and must fade.

Seeing Determinations Through the Sense of Self

B: But can't you talk about sankhāras in a more general sense and then a particular

type of sankhāra?

Nm: Yes, but why are we talking about it in the first place? Because we all want to be

free. So you don't talk about them in any other sense unless you're free. It's not

incorrect to say that sankhāras are the determination of the five aggregates, but it's

practically inaccurate.

What I'm saying is, if you have no other reference for what sankhāras are, then what

I just described is good enough. Now that you can have a more accurate reference,

the one I'm showing you, the others become redundant.

So that's the point. Where are the things that I own? Why do I own them? Because

you don't realize you can't own them. Hence, ignorance determines the sense of

ownership. Ignorance is avijjā-paccayā sankhārā. That's it. It's practical; it's

nothing abstract. And it's certainly not the observation of tingling in your knees.

You can observe all you want and try to manage your responses to whatever's

happening. But fundamentally, you're fully appropriating the body as a whole. So you

can dismiss the legs, you can dismiss everything, but overall, it's still assumed as my

body.

To find what sankhāras are, begin by looking for something which is dear to you,

something you care about. You have to start on that emotional basis and then see

what that emotional basis depends upon. Then look at the characteristics of that, as

we described, as unownable. That's what sankhāras are. Hence, you understand

what sankhāras are, you're not ignorant of them, and that means there are no

sankhāras that determine you as you anymore. Because it requires you to not know

that in order to maintain it.

B: And it's always a background phenomenon.

Nm:  Of course. It's what the mind does. So when people say, Oh, these sankhāras

are in my knees...

Would that knee be your knee if you had already relinquished the ownership of the

entire body as a whole? If you fully understood what this body is liable to, what these

elements will go back to, that they can't be yours to begin with. This body cannot be

yours even if you want it to. The only way to maintain it as yours is to ignore the fact

that it can't be yours.

And people ignore that fact when they refuse to think about death, sickness, aging,

losing their loved ones, and so on. That's how you ignore it, because it's unpleasant to

think about. So, if you do start reflecting like that and genuinely develop complete

dispassion towards your own body, would you still be concerned about a random

tingling sensation in your knee? No, because you've already abandoned the whole

domain of appropriation. What difference does it make?

## Existential Anxiety

It needs to start with that personal concern, and through that concern, you can get to

understand it. The plurality of sankhāras is seen through the singularity of the sense

of self. That's how you can see it rightly. It's not: ...that's impermanent, that's

impermanent, that's impermanent, thus I am not myself. No. I am myself, and then

I see I cannot be my own because I am not my own master. I am not in control of my

own life. So within that sense of I am, you then find all these other things that are

determining you, and you realize they are unownable.

B: The self-determination undermines itself.

Nm: Yes. But that's an important point, because people could understand this as,

Okay, so you just find these random things and then reflect on how they cannot be

yours. That's accurate in theory, but in practice, you want to do that through your

sense of self, not left, right, up, or down.

So first, you have to start in the right order. I am such-and-such in this situation.

There is obviously some emotional engagement, something felt. And that's the basis

through which you start looking at things that are dear to you.

B: So those determinations are always seen in relation to this sense of self.

Nm: Those things will be seen as determinations only through the sense of self.

Everything can be a determination if your sense of self depends on it. Which means,

in order to see it as a determination, you need to see it through your sense of self.

In other words, seeing that the context of self is determined by the content that I am

taking as pertaining to me, to mine. Seeing that relationship undermines the

ignorance of that situation.

The sense of self depends on certain contents. This is mine, this is my definition, my

belonging. First, you have to start in the right order. (Mn35) I am a king; that's

determined by my royal things. Not, I am a king that has all of this. Because that's, I

am; this is mine. It's the other way around. I am because there are things that are

mine through that sense of I am.
