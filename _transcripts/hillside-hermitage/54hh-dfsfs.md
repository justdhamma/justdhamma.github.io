<!-- # Stop Wanting the Wanting of Sensuality -->

(Hillside Hermitage Talk no.54hh - edited transcript)

Summary: The abandonment of sensuality requires developing a universal attitude of not-wanting toward the inherent wanting of sense objects, so as to realize an imperturbable peace.

## Defining Sensuality and the Path to its Abandonment

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** Wanting sensual objects—or rather, the wanting of objects from which you derive pleasure on the level of the senses—that is what sensuality is. It's that wanting.

So, to abandon sensuality, you have to stop being concerned with the objects that you want. Instead, you must start developing a "not-wanting" of that very wanting for the objects of the senses. Of course, restraint needs to be established first. On the basis of that restraint, you can begin to discern the nature of wanting anything sensual. This isn't just about this or that manifestation; it aims at anything that gives you sensual pleasure: sights, sounds, smells, tastes, and touches.

As long as you remain focused only on the objects you want, you are failing to see the root of that sensuality.

## Overcoming the Wanting, Not Just the Objects

Consequently, if you develop "not-wanting" in regard to the wanting of sense objects, that is how you overcome the sense objects themselves. You lose concern for them because you have uprooted them by developing a not-wanting of the wanting for them.

Sensuality is the wanting of sensual objects. To "welcome" sensuality, as the Buddha would say—to entertain it and delight in it—is to accept that wanting of sense objects at face value. A skilled monk, a person who practices correctly, will first be restrained. Then, they will start seeing that it is the wanting that is the problem, not the sensual objects.

The wanting is in regard to the sensual objects. So, that is what he should not welcome, entertain, or delight in. By not welcoming, entertaining, and delighting in the wanting of the sensual objects, you surmount the whole domain of sensuality, which consists of both the sensual objects and the wanting of them.

That is what I mean when I say, "develop the not-wanting of the wanting of sensual objects." It means do not delight, do not entertain, do not welcome the wanting of sensual objects.

**The Misapplication of Effort**

A person who doesn't see this will be concerned with the sensual objects themselves—denying them, telling themselves, "It's ugly, it's impermanent." They are essentially misapplying their efforts toward the sense objects, failing to see that the problem is rooted in their being wanted. The objects themselves are not the problem.

This person will then try to override that initial wanting of sensual objects, try to get rid of it, and try to prevent it from arising. But you can't do that, because that's not up to you. As the Buddha said, the eye wants to see pleasant sights; the nose wants to smell, the ear wants to hear, the body wants to touch. That is just the structure of your experience; those are the five senses and how they operate. This is what is referred to as their "feeding grounds."

To interfere with that innate wanting of your sense objects is to take it up as well. That is a common misunderstanding of the teaching to not delight, welcome, or entertain it. It does not mean you deny its first arising. It means you see the whole situation: you see the sense objects that are being wanted by the senses, and you recognize *that* is what you don't delight in. You don't want to entertain the wanting of sensuality.

**The Unwanted Visitor: An Analogy for Practice**

If the wanting of sensuality has arisen, it will persist on its own as an arisen phenomenon for as long as it persists. Your only concern is to not delight in it, not welcome it, and not entertain it at any point during its duration. At every moment, you remain firm: "No, I don't want that." It remains unwanted.

It's like someone comes to your front door and wants to come in, but the only way they can enter is if you welcome them and let them in. In the beginning, you might feel awkward, pressured, and nervous because they are right there. You might either go out to them or welcome them in out of weakness. But if you realize, "Wait a minute, they *cannot* come in," they could stand there for a hundred years if they wanted. That is not your concern. Your concern is to not welcome them inside. That's it.

And if you practice like that, it will go away on its own. It's like Mara's daughters; they will try to harass you for as long as they perceive a way in—a possibility of being allowed inside. That's what they tried with the Buddha after his enlightenment, but there was nothing; they couldn't get in, and Mara told them off. For those who don't practice correctly, the mere presence at the door is enough for them to cave in and engage. But if you recognize, "No, all I have to do is keep the door closed"—not try to

**The Nature of Sensuality's "Reward"**

Regardless of the sight, sound, smell, taste, or touch—whether past, present, or future—you are not concerned with its content. You simply maintain the attitude of not-wanting toward the arisen wanting for these sense objects. That's it.

If you do that long enough, the arising of the wanting for sense objects will change its significance. It will no longer be about the objects, but about, "Oh, this is that which is not welcomed. This is that which is not delighted in. This is that which used to be my sensuality." "Used to be" meant you went after these objects because they were wanted, and you took that wanting at face value. Or, you went after their absence because they were not wanted, and you took that at face value. But now, you stop welcoming the wanting of the sense object.

The whole domain remains the same; the eyes still have their feeding grounds, but now you have conquered them. The eye will get to eat only what the mind—which wants none of it—allows. And whatever it allows, it won't be a sensual thing, because that has been abandoned.

Sensuality requires taking the wanting for granted. When you stop wanting the wanting, you get to see the full extent of it—its gratification and its danger. By not wanting the wanting of sense objects, you understand the danger inherent within it. For example, if you say "yes" to wanting a pleasant sight and in the next ten seconds you go blind or the sight is ripped away, you will be affected. But if you had never given in to the original wanting, would you be affected? You wouldn't, because you never welcomed it in.

This is the nature of sensuality. The directionality of wanting sense objects always takes you one way or the other, which means you cannot maintain perspective; you must commit and go with it. This means you are not in control.

Ask yourself: which is more common, the times I got what I wanted or the times I didn't? You realize it's the times I didn't. But if you stop wanting any type of wanting for sense objects, you cannot not get what you want, because you only want one thing: non-sensuality. Sensuality then keeps reminding you of your freedom from it.

**The Inherent Danger**

You can realize that sensuality is inherently unpleasant, dangerous, and offers very little gratification—not worth the effort. It's like being asked to pay $1,000 for a

Factually, that's the deal. For the thousand pieces you invest, you get one in return. You realize, "Wait a minute. All I need to do is not invest in this, and I'll gain a thousandfold." All I need to do is not do sensuality, and I win. That is the pleasure of jhana, that relief. Because sensuality is of such little gratification and great danger, once you understand it, you will abandon it. You cannot understand the nature of sensuality correctly and still want it; the two are mutually exclusive. Understanding it means not wanting it.

That's why the Buddha says the person who engages in sensuality is one who seeks happiness. That's where people start. The practice is to use that same criteria: I want pleasure. But then you reflect: which is more pleasant? Investing a thousand to maybe get one back, or refraining from investing and keeping it all? The second one is more pleasant. So even from the motivation of wanting pleasure, you will turn away from sensuality.

However, the not-wanting of the wanting of sense objects needs to be universal. That attitude must be developed not just for today or tomorrow, but for the rest of my life and any lives that may come. The wanting of sense objects will never be wanted, will never be welcomed. It might arise a million more times, but every time it arises, it is not welcomed, and you do not open the door.

Eventually, it will have to go away. Either way, you will win. You have to convince Mara and his daughters that the door will never be opened for them, ever again.
