Mental Resistance and Dispassion
The reason why death, and the pains of dying, are so unpleasant is because of the
confinement that the mind naturally resists and craves against. Not because it's

## Sambādhasutta: Confinement
## The Deva's Verse
On one occasion, Venerable Ānanda was dwelling at Kosambī in Ghosita’s Park.
Then, Venerable Udāyī approached Venerable Ānanda and exchanged greetings with
him. After some polite and amiable conversation, he sat to one side and said:
"Friend, the young deva Pañcālacaṇḍa said this:
'The one of vast wisdom has found an opening in confinement [crowdedness /
restriction];
The awakened one who awakened to jhāna,
The withdrawn chief sage.'
What is the confinement, and what is the finding of an opening in confinement
spoken of by the Blessed One?"
The Cords of Kāma
"These five cords of kāma are the confinement spoken of by the Blessed One. What
five?
Forms cognizable by eye-viññāṇa that are desirable, lovely, pleasing, agreeable,
accompanied by kāma, and enticing.
Sounds cognizable by ear-viññāṇa...
Odors cognizable by nose-viññāṇa...
Tastes cognizable by tongue-viññāṇa...

These five cords of kāma are the confinement spoken of by the Blessed One.
## The First Jhāna
"Here, a bhikkhu, withdrawn from kāma and withdrawn from unbeneficial dhammā,
enters and remains in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by vitakka and vicara,
and possesses pīti and sukha born of that viveka.
To this extent, the finding of an opening in confinement was spoken of by the Blessed
One provisionally [in a relative sense]. Yet there is still confinement there. And what
is the confinement there? Whatever vitakka and vicara have not ceased there, that is
the confinement here.
## The Second Jhāna
"Furthermore, with the stilling of vitakka and vicara, a bhikkhu enters and remains
in the second jhāna, which has internal confidence and collectedness of citta, is
without vitakka and vicara, and has pīti and sukha born of that samādhi.
To this extent, the finding of an opening in confinement was spoken of by the Blessed
One provisionally. Yet there is still confinement there. And what is the confinement
there? Whatever pīti has not ceased there, that is the confinement here.
## The Third Jhāna
"Furthermore, with dispassion for pīti, he dwells with upekkhā, sati, clear

To this extent, the finding of an opening in confinement was spoken of by the Blessed
One provisionally. Yet there is still confinement there. And what is the confinement
there? Whatever upekkhā-sukha has not ceased there, that is the confinement here.

## Saṁyutta Nikāya 2.7 Pañcālacaṇḍasutta: Pañcālacaṇḍa
Originating at Sāvatthī. Standing to one side, the devaputta Pañcālacaṇḍa recited this
verse in the presence of the Blessed One:
## The Devaputta's Verse
"Amidst confinement, the one of vast wisdom has indeed found an opening.

The Worldling and the Noble Disciple
Thus have I heard: On one occasion, the Buddha was staying in Rājagaha at the
## Kalandaka Bamboo Grove.
At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the assembly:
"Foolish, untaught worldlings experience dukkha vedanā, sukha vedanā, and
neither-dukkha-nor-sukha vedanā. The well-taught ariyasāvako also experiences
## 7

## 8
dukkha vedanā, sukha vedanā, and neither-dukkha-nor-sukha vedanā. What, then,
is the difference between the common worldling and the noble person?"
They addressed the Buddha: "The World-Honored One is the root of the dhammā,
the eye of the dhammā, and the refuge of the dhammā. Excellent! World-Honored
One, we only wish that you would speak extensively on this. Having heard it, we will
receive, uphold, and practice it."
The Double Arrow of the Worldling
The Buddha told them: "When the foolish, untaught worldling's kāya makes phassa,
various vedanā arise. Oppressed by the pain of dukkha—even to the point of losing
their life—they sorrow, grieve, weep, and cry out, wailing in resentment and
complaint.
"Listen carefully and consider it well; I will speak to you. When the foolish, untaught
worldling's kāya makes phassa and various vedanā arise, the pain of dukkha
increases, even to the point of taking their life. Sorrowing, grieving, complaining, and
resenting, they weep, cry, and wail, and their citta falls into madness and confusion.
Right at that time, two kinds of vedanā increase: kāya vedanā and citta vedanā.
"Suppose a man's kāya is struck by two poisoned arrows, and extreme pain and
dukkha arise. The foolish, untaught worldling is exactly like this. Two vedanā—kāya
vedanā and citta vedanā—increase, and extreme pain and dukkha arise.
"Why is this so? Because that foolish, untaught worldling does not truly understand.
When making phassa with the five objects of kāma, a sukha vedanā arises. Because
they experience the sukha of the five kāma, they are driven by the underlying
tendency of rāga. Because they make phassa with a dukkha vedanā, dosa arises.
Because dosa arises, they are driven by the underlying tendency of dosa.
"Regarding these two vedanā, they do not truly know their origination, cessation,
gratification, danger, and escape as they really are. Because they do not know them
as they really are, neither-dukkha-nor-sukha vedanā arises, and they are driven by
the underlying tendency of moha.
## 8

## 9
"Bound by sukha vedanā, they ultimately do not escape it. Bound by dukkha vedanā,
they ultimately do not escape it. Bound by neither-dukkha-nor-sukha vedanā, they
ultimately do not escape it. By what are they bound? They are bound by rāga, dosa,
and moha. They are bound by birth, aging, illness, death, sorrow, lamentation,
despair, and dukkha.
The Single Arrow of the Noble Disciple
"When the well-taught ariyasāvako's kāya makes phassa, a dukkha vedanā arises.
Though oppressed by great dukkha—even to the point of taking their life—they do
not give rise to sorrow, lamentation, complaints, or resentment. They do not weep,
cry, or wail, nor does their citta become confused or go mad. Right at that time, only
one vedanā arises: namely, kāya vedanā. Citta vedanā does not arise.
"Suppose a man is struck by a single poisoned arrow but is not struck by a second
poisoned arrow. Right at that time, only one vedanā arises: namely, kāya vedanā.
Citta vedanā does not arise.
"When making phassa with a sukha vedanā, they are not defiled by the sukha of
kāma. Because they are not defiled by the sukha of kāma, the underlying tendency of
rāga toward that sukha vedanā does not drive them. When experiencing dukkha
phassa vedanā, dosa does not arise. Because dosa does not arise, the underlying
tendency of dosa does not drive them.
"Regarding those two underlying tendencies, they know the origin, cessation,
gratification, danger, and escape as they really are. Because they know them as they
really are, the underlying tendency of moha toward neither-dukkha-nor-sukha
vedanā does not drive them. Regarding that sukha vedanā, it is liberated and
unbound. Regarding the dukkha vedanā and the neither-dukkha-nor-sukha vedanā,
it is liberated and unbound.
"From what is it unbound? It is unbound from rāga, dosa, and moha. It is unbound
from birth, aging, illness, death, sorrow, lamentation, despair, and dukkha."

## Saṁyutta Nikāya 36.6 Sallasutta: The Arrow
The Two Kinds of Experience
"The uninstructed worldling vedayati a sukha vedanā, vedayati a dukkha vedanā,
and vedayati an adukkhamasukha vedanā. The instructed ariyasāvako also
vedayati a sukha vedanā, vedayati a dukkha vedanā, and vedayati an
adukkhamasukha vedanā. What, then, is the distinction, the disparity, and the
difference between the instructed ariyasāvako and the uninstructed worldling?"
"Venerable sir, our dhammā are rooted in the Blessed One..."
## 10

## 11
The Worldling and the Second Arrow
"When the uninstructed worldling is touched by a dukkha vedanā, they sorrow,
languish, lament, cry beating their breast, and undergo bewilderment. They vedayati
two vedanās: kāyika and cetasika.
It is just as if they were to pierce a man with an arrow [dart], and then they were to
pierce him with a second arrow following it. Thus, that man would vedayati the
vedanā of two arrows. In exactly the same way, when the uninstructed worldling is
touched by a dukkha vedanā, they sorrow, languish, lament, cry beating their breast,
and undergo bewilderment. They vedayati two vedanās: kāyika and cetasika.
When they are touched by that very dukkha vedanā, they have aversion/resistance
toward it. For one who has aversion toward a dukkha vedanā, whatever underlying
tendency to aversion there is toward dukkha vedanā underlies them. When they are
touched by a dukkha vedanā, they delight in kāmasukha. Why is that? Because the
uninstructed worldling does not understand any escape [release] from dukkha
vedanā other than kāmasukha. As they delight in kāmasukha, whatever rāgānusayo
there is toward sukha vedanā underlies them. They do not understand the origin
[arising], the passing away [subsiding], the gratification [enjoyment], the danger
[drawback], and the escape from these vedanās as it actually is. As they do not
understand this, whatever avijjānusayo there is toward adukkhamasukha vedanā
underlies them.
If they vedayati a sukha vedanā, they vedayati tied to it. If they vedayati a dukkha
vedanā, they vedayati tied to it. If they vedayati an adukkhamasukha vedanā, they
vedayati tied to it. This is called an uninstructed worldling who is tied to birth, aging,
and death, to sorrows, laments, dukkhas, domanassas, and despairs; they are tied to
dukkha, I say.
The Noble Disciple and the Single Arrow
When the instructed ariyasāvako is touched by a dukkha vedanā, they do not
sorrow, languish, lament, cry beating their breast, or undergo bewilderment. They
vedayati one vedanā: kāyika, not cetasika.

## Pali Definitions:
## 1. Adukkhamasukha
● Etymology: a- (not) + dukkha (pain/unease) + ma (sandhi/linking letter) +
sukha (pleasure/ease).
## ● Literal Meaning: Not-painful-not-pleasant.
## 2. Arahant / Arahantship
● Etymology: From the root arah- (to be worthy) or a compound of ari
(enemy) + hant (killer).
● Literal Meaning: The worthy one; or the destroyer of enemies
## (defilements).
## 3. Ariyasāvako
● Etymology: ariya (noble) + sāvaka (hearer/listener, from the root śru, to
hear).
● Literal Meaning: Noble listener; Noble disciple.
- Āsava (Āsavas)
● Etymology: ā- (towards/into) + the root su (to flow or press out).
● Literal Meaning: Influx, outflow, fermentation, or leak.
## 5. Avijjānusayo
● Etymology: a- (not) + vijjā (knowledge) + anusaya (from anu- [along] +
root si [to lie down]).
● Literal Meaning: The underlying tendency of non-knowledge or ignoring
(ignore: in-(not), gnar-(knowing))
## 13

## 14
## 6. Bhava
● Etymology: From the root bhū (to be, to become).
● Literal Meaning: Existence, being.
## 7. Bhikkhu
● Etymology: From the root bhikkh- (to beg or ask for a share).
● Literal Meaning: Mendicant; one who relies on shared food.
## 8. Cetasika
● Etymology: Derived from citta + the adjectival suffix -ika.
● Literal Meaning: Pertaining to the mind.
## 9. Citta
● Etymology: From the root cit (to think, perceive, or be aware).
## ● Literal Meaning: Mind.
## 10. Deva / Devaputta
● Etymology: From the root div (to shine). Putta means son or child.
● Literal Meaning: Shining one; Son of a shining one.
## 11. Saṅkhātadhamma
● Etymology: sam- (together) + the root khā (to measure, count, reckon) +
dhamma (nature/phenomena).

● Literal Meaning: One who has taken the measure of phenomena.
## 12. Domanassa
● Etymology: du- (bad/difficult) + manas (intellect,mental).
● Literal Meaning: Bad-mindedness; mental pain or displeasure.
## 13. Dosa
● Etymology: From the root duṣ (to spoil, corrupt, or be bad).
● Literal Meaning: Spoiling, corruption, aversion, anger.
## 14. Dukkha
● Etymology: du- (bad/difficult) + kha (hole, space, or axle-hole).
## 14

## 15
● Literal Meaning: Hard to bear; a bad axle-hole (implying friction and a
bumpy ride); unease.
## 15. Jhāna
● Etymology: From the root dhyā or jhā (to burn, to contemplate, to
comprehend).
● Literal Meaning: Burning (up of defilements); composed comprehension.
## 16. Kāma / Kāmasukha
● Etymology: From the root kam (to desire, wish, or love).
● Literal Meaning: Sensuality, desire, or longing. Kāmasukha = the pleasure
of sensuality.
## 17. Kāya / Kāyika
● Etymology: From the root ci (to heap, pile up, or accumulate).
● Literal Meaning: A heap, a collection, a body. Kāyika = bodily / pertaining

## 21. Pīti

## 22. Phassa

## 23. Rāga / Rāgānusayo

## 25. Saññā
● Etymology: sam- (together) + the root jñā (to know).
● Literal Meaning: Knowing together, perception, recognition, or sign.
## 26. Sati
● Etymology: From the root smṛ (to remember or recollect).
● Literal Meaning: Memory, recollection, bearing in mind.
## 27. Somanassa
● Etymology: su- (good/easy) + manas (intellect, mental).

the hearing" of the Dhamma.

## 29. Sukha

## 30. Upekkhā
