# **Do We Live Only Once**

*(Talk no.04hh - condensed transcript )*

[Youtube](https://youtu.be/QXdTop65GTQ?feature=shared) - [suttas.hillsidehermitage](https://suttas.hillsidehermitage.org/?q=an3.65) - [suttacentral](https://suttacentral.net/an4.62)

**Q:** Some people say, "You only live once, so you should enjoy life to the fullest. Do whatever makes
you happy!" What do you say to that idea?

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** Well, for many people, that phrase is just a form of dismissal. "You only live
once!" And there's nothing you can say to them. But if someone is genuinely questioning that idea, the
conversation can begin. The simple fact is, you don't really know how many times you live.

Some people believe you live only once. Others believe you live many times. Some believe you live
twice - a rebirth in heaven or hell, and that's it. The point is, it will always be a matter of belief.

So, if you want to develop yourself in an ultimate way, you must first recognize and admit this
uncertainty. To be completely confident in saying "you only live once" isn't based on truth, because
you don't know for sure. Being certain about a mere belief is a big mistake and a very risky thing to
do.

I'm not saying you should abandon beliefs. I'm saying you should recognize them *as* beliefs. From
there, you can ask, "What is the safest belief to hold?"

Think about it. If you believe you only live once and behave however you want, what happens if
you're wrong? What if you live more than once? You could end up paying the price for your behavior
in this life and lives to come. Those who believe in future lives - Christians, Buddhists, Hindus - all
believe you will be reborn according to your actions. For Christians, if you aren't a good person, the
doors to heaven will be closed. The belief in more than one life requires a certain degree of valuing
good behavior, because if you don't behave well, your future lives will be compromised.

So, what is the safest route? It's to believe there are probably more lives, just in case. You don't need
to look for proof; you just have to choose the belief that is safest in the long run. If there are no more
lives, so be it. You lived a good life, people praised and trusted you. You're rewarded in *this* life with
respect, friendships and good opportunities. If there *are* future lives, you'll be rewarded even more. It's
a win-win.

“...When that noble disciple has a mind that’s thus free of enmity and ill will, undefiled and purified,
they’ve won four consolations right in the present experience. ‘If it turns out there is another world,
and good and bad actions have a result, then—when the body breaks up, after death—I’ll reappear in
a good place, a heavenly realm.’ This is the first consolation they’ve won.

‘If it turns out there is no other world, and good and bad actions don’t have a result, then in the
present life I’ll keep myself free of enmity and ill will, untroubled and at ease.’ This is the second
consolation they’ve won.

---

**2**

‘If it turns out that bad things happen to one who does bad things, then I still don’t do anything bad.
Since I don’t do anything bad, how can suffering touch me?’ This is the third consolation they’ve
won.

‘If it turns out that bad things don’t happen to one who does bad things, then I still see myself as pure
in both respects.’ This is the fourth consolation they’ve won.

When that noble disciple has a mind that’s thus free of enmity and ill will, undefiled and purified,
they’ve won these four consolations right in the present experience.” - An3.65

Now, let's look at that other part of the idea: "Do whatever makes you happy." The problem is, most
people don't actually know what makes them happy. For them, "happiness" is simply the absence of
unhappiness. This means their pursuit of happiness becomes a constant, active flight from the prospect
of unpleasant feelings. Their whole life revolves around being chased by unhappiness and anxiety, and
their goal is simply not to get caught.

If you look, that's exactly how most people live. It's why we see so many mental health issues in
society - too much pressure, too much stress. People turn to medication, vices, and other extreme
measures because they don't know what to do. They've embraced this escape at face value. But when
"doing what makes you happy" just means preventing unhappiness at all costs, sensuality becomes
one of the only ways to get relief. That isn't true happiness. It's just managing your unhappiness.

A true, lasting happiness is one you don't have to constantly maintain. It's the happiness of freedom
itself - the feeling of being above the things that can affect you. It's the happiness of not being chased.

The happiness you feel when you're running away is just a temporary relief, like when you think
you're momentarily safe from a beast that's chasing you. You might feel relief from the imminent
threat, but you're not actually free from the beast. That is only a relative happiness. And that beast is
any form of suffering: anxiety, fear, stress, loss. The mind's default reaction is to turn away from
suffering, and when that becomes the purpose of your life, you're just running.

That's why people compulsively give in to sensual desire. It isn't optional for them; it's almost
mandatory. They've chosen the path of running away, which means they can't resist their desires. They
become bound by fear and the endless escape from suffering. Any happiness experienced within that
trap is, by definition, a meager happiness.

# **Aṅguttara Nikāya 4.62**

**The Discourse on Debtlessness**

Then the householder Anāthapiṇḍika went to the Blessed One, paid his respects, and sat down to one
side. The Blessed One then said this to Anāthapiṇḍika, who was sitting there:

“Householder, there are four kinds of happiness that a layperson who enjoys sensual pleasures can
rightly attain from time to time. What are the four? The happiness of ownership, the happiness of
enjoyment, the happiness of debtlessness, and the happiness of blamelessness.

---

**3**

And what, householder, is the happiness of ownership? It is when a person possesses wealth acquired
through energetic effort, gathered by the strength of their own arms, earned by the sweat of their
brow—wealth that is legitimate and has been obtained in a righteous way. They reflect, ‘I have wealth
that I acquired through my own effort and earned righteously,’ and this brings them happiness and joy.
This, householder, is called the happiness of ownership.

And what, householder, is the happiness of enjoyment? It is when a person, using the wealth they
have earned righteously, both enjoys it and performs meritorious deeds. They reflect, ‘With the wealth
I have earned, I am both enjoying my life and performing good deeds,’ and this brings them happiness
and joy. This, householder, is called the happiness of enjoyment.

And what, householder, is the happiness of debtlessness? It is when a person owes nothing to anyone,
whether the amount is small or large. They reflect, ‘I owe nothing to anyone, small or large,’ and this
brings them happiness and joy. This, householder, is called the happiness of debtlessness.

And what, householder, is the happiness of blamelessness? It is when a noble disciple is endowed
with blameless conduct of body, blameless conduct of speech, and blameless conduct of mind. They
reflect, ‘I am blameless in my actions, words, and thoughts,’ and this brings them happiness and joy.
This, householder, is called the happiness of blamelessness.

These, householder, are the four kinds of happiness that a layperson who enjoys sensual pleasures can
rightly attain from time to time.”

Having known the joy of being free from debt,
And the joy that comes from ownership,
A person can enjoy their wealth;
Through this, wisdom sees with clarity.

Seeing clearly, the wise person understands both kinds of wealth,
But knows the material joys are not worth even a sixteenth part
Of the happiness that comes from being blameless.”

# **Aṅguttara Nikāya 3.65**

**Kesamuttisutta With the Kālāmas of Kesamutta**

So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was wandering in the land of the Kosalans together with a
large Saṅgha of bhikkhus when he arrived at a town of the Kālāmas named Kesamutta. The Kālāmas
of Kesamutta heard:

“It seems the ascetic Gotama—a Sakyan, gone forth from a Sakyan family—has arrived at Kesamutta.
He has this good reputation: ‘That Auspicious One is an Arahant, a fully awakened Buddha …’ It’s
good to see such Arahants.”

Then the Kālāmas went up to the Buddha. Before sitting down to one side, some bowed, some
exchanged pleasantries, some held up their joined palms toward the Buddha, some announced their
name and clan, while some kept silent. Seated to one side the Kālāmas said to the Buddha:

---

**4**

“Bhante, there are some ascetics and brahmins who come to Kesamutta. They explain and promote
only their own doctrine, while they attack, deride, disparage, and smear the doctrines of others. Then
some other ascetics and brahmins come to Kesamutta. They too explain and promote only their own
doctrine, while they attack, deride, disparage, and smear the doctrines of others. So, Bhante, we’re
doubting and uncertain: ‘I wonder who of these respected ascetics and brahmins speaks the truth, and
who speaks falsehood?’”

“It is appropriate, Kālāmas, for you to be doubting and uncertain. Doubt has come up in you about an
uncertain matter.

Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission, by lineage, by hearsay, by canonical authority, by logic, by
inference, by analysis of properties, by the acceptance of a view after consideration, by the appearance
of competence, or by the thought ‘The ascetic is our respected teacher.’ When you know for
yourselves: ‘These things are unbeneficial, blameworthy, criticized by the wise, and when undertaken,
[they lead to harm and suffering’, then you should give them up. ](https://suttas.hillsidehermitage.org?q=an3.65#comment2)

What do you think, Kālāmas? When greed arises in a person internally, does it do so for their welfare
or harm?”

“Harm, Bhante.”

“A greedy individual, overcome by greed, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery, lies, and
encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?”

“Yes, Bhante.”

“What do you think, Kālāmas? When aversion arises in a person internally, does it do so for their
welfare or harm?”

“Harm, Bhante.”

“A hateful individual, overcome by aversion, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery, lies, and
encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?”

“Yes, Bhante.”

“What do you think, Kālāmas? When muddledness arises in a person internally, does it do so for their
welfare or harm?”

“Harm, Bhante.”

“A muddled individual, overcome by muddledness, kills living creatures, steals, commits adultery,
lies, and encourages others to do the same. Is that for their lasting harm and suffering?”

“Yes, Bhante.”

“What do you think, Kālāmas, are these things beneficial or unbeneficial?”

“Unbeneficial, Bhante.”

“Blameworthy or blameless?”

---

**5**

“Blameworthy, Bhante.”

“Criticized or praised by the wise?”

“Criticized by the wise, Bhante.”

“When undertaken, do they lead to harm and suffering, or not? Or how do you see this?”

“When undertaken, they lead to harm and suffering, Bhante. That’s how we see it.”

“So, Kālāmas, when I said: ‘Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission, by lineage, by hearsay, by
canonical authority, by logic, by inference, by analysis of properties, by the acceptance of a view after
consideration, by the appearance of competence, or by the thought ‘The ascetic is our respected
teacher.’ But when you know for yourselves: ‘These things are unbeneficial, blameworthy, criticized
by the wise, and when undertaken, they lead to harm and suffering’, then you should give them up.
That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.

Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission,  by lineage, by hearsay, by canonical authority, by logic, by
inference, by analysis of properties, by the acceptance of a view after consideration, by the appearance
of competence, or by the thought ‘The ascetic is our respected teacher.’ But when you know for
yourselves: ‘These things are beneficial, blameless, praised by the wise, and when undertaken, they
lead to welfare and ease’, then you should abide having entered upon them.

What do you think, Kālāmas? When non-greed arises in a person internally, does it do so for their
welfare or harm?”

“Welfare, Bhante.”

“An individual who is not greedy, not overcome by greed, doesn’t kill living creatures, steal, commit
adultery, lie, or encourage others to do the same. Is that for their lasting welfare and happiness?”

“Yes, Bhante.”

“What do you think, Kālāmas? When non-aversion arises in a person internally, does it do so for their
welfare or harm? … When non-muddledness arises in a person internally, does it do so for their
welfare or harm? … Is that for their lasting welfare and happiness?”

“Yes, Bhante.”

“What do you think, Kālāmas, are these things beneficial or unbeneficial?”

“Beneficial, Bhante.”

“Blameworthy or blameless?”

“Blameless, Bhante.”

“Criticized or praised by the wise?”

“Praised by the wise, Bhante.”

---

**6**

“When you undertake them, do they lead to welfare and happiness, or not? Or how do you see this?”

“When you undertake them, they lead to welfare and happiness. That’s how we see it.”

“So, Kālāmas, when I said: ‘Kālāmas, don’t go by oral transmission,  by lineage, by hearsay, by
canonical authority, by logic, by inference, by analysis of properties, by the acceptance of a view after
consideration, by the appearance of competence, or by the thought ‘The ascetic is our respected
teacher.’ But when you know for yourselves:

“These things are beneficial, blameless, praised by the wise, and when undertaken, they lead to
welfare and ease”, then you should abide having entered upon them.’ That’s what I said, and this is
why I said it.

Then that noble disciple is rid of longing, rid of ill will, unmuddled, aware, and recollected. He abides
having pervaded with a mind of friendliness the first direction, likewise second, likewise third,
likewise the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, he abides having pervaded the
whole world with a mind of friendliness—abundant, expansive, immeasurable, free of enmity and ill
will.

He abides having pervaded with a mind of compassion …

He abides having pervaded with a mind of contentment …

He abides having pervaded with a mind of equanimity the first direction, likewise the second, likewise
the third, likewise the fourth. In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, he abides having
pervaded the whole world with a mind of equanimity—abundant, expansive, immeasurable, free of
enmity and ill will.

When that noble disciple has a mind that’s thus free of enmity and ill will, undefiled and purified,
they’ve won four consolations right in the present experience. ‘If it turns out there is another world,
and good and bad actions have a result, then—when the body breaks up, after death—I’ll reappear in
a good place, a heavenly realm.’ This is the first consolation they’ve won.

‘If it turns out there is no other world, and good and bad actions don’t have a result, then in the
present life I’ll keep myself free of enmity and ill will, untroubled and at ease.’ This is the second
consolation they’ve won.

‘If it turns out that bad things happen to one who does bad things, then I still don’t do anything bad.
Since I don’t do anything bad, how can suffering touch me?’ This is the third consolation they’ve
won.

‘If it turns out that bad things don’t happen to one who does bad things, then I still see myself pure in
both respects.’ This is the fourth consolation they’ve won.

When that noble disciple has a mind that’s thus free of enmity and ill will, undefiled and purified,
they’ve won these four consolations right in the present experience.”

“Thus it is, Auspicious One! Thus it is, Excellent One! When that noble disciple has a mind that’s thus
free of enmity and ill will, undefiled and purified, they’ve won four consolations right in the present
experience. …

---

**7**

Excellent, Bhante! Excellent! … We go for refuge to Master Gotama, to the teaching, and to the
bhikkhu Saṅgha. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember us as lay followers who have gone
for refuge for life.”

Translated by Bhikkhu Anīgha
