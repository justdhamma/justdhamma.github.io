<!-- ## How To Overcome Attachments -->

[suttas.hillsidehermitage](https://suttas.hillsidehermitage.org/?q=mn54#mn54:0.1_mn54:0.2)

**Q:** How can I be free from attachment?  

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** Well, the first step is to start seeing it not as a source of pleasure but of danger.

All you have to do to start undoing those attachments, say in terms of being attached to your house, is to start pondering, thinking of the very real possibility of losing it through natural catastrophes or anything else. Such things that can happen are beyond your control and can happen at any time. You can then realize that when those things get swept away with you attached to them that's very dangerous.

When you recognize that perilous situation you are in, you will want to not be attached to those
things.

**Q:** But must you throw those things that you are attached to away?

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** No, destroying those things does not free you from attachment. The attachment is not the things;
it's your attitude in regard to them.

Now, the most natural response is when a person says, "Oh, I'm attached to this," they'll throw it away.
"I'll break it apart. I'll say no to it." But that doesn't affect your mind or your attitude of attachment,
which means another thing will slot in instead of it, sooner or later. You're going to give in.

So, in order to free yourself from attachment, you need to discern the reasons for your attachment.
And things are not the reasons; they are the objects of your attachment. The reasons are fear for your
life, fear for your safety, fear for your health, fear of boredom, fear of not having anything to do, fear
of loneliness - all those things that people are afraid of. And that's why they attach to other people and
to other things.

That's what you want to overcome: those fears, and not act out of them. And the very first step to
overcoming any fear is being aware of it, admitting it, discerning it.

**Q:** You can't tell from somebody's outward appearance that they are free from attachment.

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** No, you can't. A person can, theoretically, be living in a very nice house, have a very
comfortable life, have lots of luxuries, but internally and genuinely not be attached to it. However, it's
highly unlikely today that that would be the case because it takes a lot of effort. And if a person is able
to make that effort and gain that wisdom, they wouldn't be investing all their time in maintaining such
things and luxuries. They'd probably be giving it up naturally. Not as a response of, "Oh, I'm bound by
this. I'm sort of trapped by my attachment, so I'll just destroy the objects and pretend I'm not
attached."

No, they would be overcoming it through proper understanding. They would be removing the actual
attachment, not the physical things. So then, if someone were to happen to have those things, they
wouldn't suffer on account of it.

But that doesn't mean they seek out the destruction of those things beforehand in order to prove to
themselves that they're not attached. If you need to prove to yourself that you're *not* attached, it means
you don't actually know. It means you're doubting, which means you *are* attached. If you're not free
from doubt, you can't say you're free.

So outward appearance, it's not a measure of attachment. A person can have a big family, many
children, but not actually be attached to them if he has wisdom.

**Q:** But that's an extremely rare case.

**Ajahn Nyanamoli:** ...and unlikely. He could be helping his family, being there for them, but if something happens, he
won't be distraught and completely unable to continue his life.

# **Middle Discourses 54: Potaliya Sutta**

> ## With Potaliya the Householder  
> So I have heard. At one time the Buddha was staying in the land of the Aṅguttarāpans, near the town of theirs named Āpaṇa.
> 
> Then the Buddha robed up in the morning and, taking his bowl and robe, entered Āpaṇa for alms. He wandered for alms in Āpaṇa. After the meal, on his return from almsround, he went to a certain forest grove for the day’s abiding. Having plunged deep into it, he sat at the root of a certain tree for the day’s abiding.
> 
> Potaliya the householder also approached that forest grove while going for a walk. He was well dressed in sarong and cloak, with parasol and sandals. Having plunged deep into it, he went up to the Buddha, and exchanged pleasantries with him. When the amicable and cordial talk was over, he stood to one side, and the Buddha said to him, “There are seats, householder. Please sit if you wish.”
> 
> When he said this, Potaliya was angry and upset. Thinking, “The ascetic Gotama addresses me as ‘householder’!” he stayed silent.
> 
> For a second time … and a third time the Buddha said to him, “There are seats, householder. Please sit if you wish.”
> 
> When he said this, Potaliya was angry and upset. Thinking, “The ascetic Gotama addresses me as ‘householder’!” he said to the Buddha, “Master Gotama, it is neither proper nor appropriate for you to address me as ‘householder’.”
> 
> “Well, householder, you have the features, attributes, and signs of a householder.”
> 
> “Master Gotama, it’s because I have refused all work and cut off all business.”
> 
> “Householder, in what way have you refused all work and cut off all business?”
> 
> “Master Gotama, all the money, grain, gold, and silver I used to have has been handed over to my children as their inheritance. And in this matter I do not advise or reprimand them, but live with nothing more than food and clothes. That’s how I have refused all work and cut off all business.”
> 
> “The cutting off of business as you describe it is one thing, householder, but the cutting off of business in the noble one’s discipline is quite different.”
> 
> “But what, venerable sir, is cutting off of business in the noble one’s discipline? Venerable sir, please teach me this.”
> 
> “Well then, householder, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir,” said Potaliya.
> 
> The Buddha said this:
> 
> “Householder, these eight things lead to the cutting off of business in the noble one’s discipline. What eight? Killing living creatures should be abandoned, relying on not killing living creatures. Taking what is not given should be abandoned, relying on not taking what is not given. Lying should be abandoned, relying on truthful speech. Divisive speech should be abandoned, relying on speech that isn’t divisive. Greed and covetousness should be abandoned, relying on not being greedy and covetous. Blaming and insulting should be abandoned, relying on not blaming and not insulting. Irritability and distress should be abandoned, relying on not being irritable and distressed. Arrogance should be abandoned, relying on not being arrogant. These are the eight things—stated in brief without being analyzed in detail—that lead to the cutting off of business in the noble one’s discipline.”
> 
> “Venerable sir, please teach me these eight things in detail out of compassion.”
> 
> “Well then, householder, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir,” said Potaliya. The Buddha said this:
> 
> “‘Killing living creatures should be abandoned, relying on not killing living creatures.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s when a noble disciple reflects: ‘I am practicing to give up and cut off the fetters that might cause me to kill living creatures. But if I were to kill living creatures, because of that I would reprimand myself; wise men, after examination, would criticize me; and when my body breaks up, after death, I could expect a bad destination. And killing living creatures is itself a fetter and a hindrance. For the influxes, trouble, and affliction that would arise because of killing living creatures are not present in someone who does not kill living creatures.’ ‘Killing living creatures should be abandoned, relying on not killing living creatures.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.
> 
> ‘Taking what is not given … lying … divisive speech … greed and covetousness … blaming and insulting … irritability and distress …
> 
> Arrogance should be abandoned, relying on not being arrogant.’ That’s what I said, but why did I say it? It’s when a noble disciple reflects: ‘I am practicing to give up and cut off the fetters that might cause me to be arrogant. But if I were to be arrogant, because of that I would reprimand myself; wise men, after examination, would criticize me; and when my body breaks up, after death, I could expect a bad destination. And arrogance is itself a fetter and a hindrance. For the influxes, trouble, and affliction that would arise because of arrogance are not present in someone who is not arrogant.’ ‘Arrogance should be abandoned by not being arrogant.’ That’s what I said, and this is why I said it.
> 
> These are the eight things—stated in brief and analyzed in detail—that lead to the cutting off of business in the noble one’s discipline. But just this much does not constitute the cutting off of business in each and every respect in the noble one’s discipline.”
> 
> “But, venerable sir, how is there the cutting off of business in each and every respect in the noble one’s discipline? Venerable sir, please teach me this.”
> 
> “Well then, householder, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak.”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir,” said Potaliya. The Buddha said this:
> 
> ## 1. The Perils of Sensuality
> 
> “Householder, suppose a dog weak with hunger was hanging around a butcher’s shop. Then a deft butcher or their apprentice would toss them a skeleton scraped clean of flesh and smeared in blood. What do you think, householder? Gnawing on such a fleshless skeleton, would that dog still get rid of its hunger?”
> 
> “No, venerable sir. Why not? Because that skeleton is scraped clean of flesh and smeared in blood. That dog will eventually get weary and frustrated.”
> 
> “In the same way, a noble disciple reflects: ‘With the simile of a skeleton the Buddha said that sensuality gives little gratification and much suffering and distress, and the peril there is what’s greater.’ Having seen this as it is with right understanding, they reject equanimity based on multiplicity and develop the equanimity based on unity, where all kinds of grasping at worldly pleasures ceases without remainder.
> 
> Suppose a vulture or a crow or a hawk was to grab a scrap of meat and fly away. Other vultures, crows, and hawks would keep chasing it, pecking and clawing. What do you think, householder? If that vulture, crow, or hawk doesn’t quickly let go of that scrap of meat, wouldn’t that result in death or deadly suffering for them?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir.” …
> 
> “Suppose a man carrying a blazing grass torch was to walk against the wind. What do you think, householder? If that man doesn’t quickly let go of that blazing grass torch, wouldn’t he burn his hands or arm or other limb, resulting in death or deadly suffering for him?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir.” …
> 
> “Suppose there was a pit of glowing coals deeper than a man’s height, full of glowing coals that neither flamed nor smoked. Then a man would come along who wants to live and doesn’t want to die, who wants to be happy and recoils from pain. Then two strong men would grab him by the arms and drag him towards the pit of glowing coals. What do you think, householder? Wouldn’t that man writhe and struggle to and fro?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir. Why is that? For that man knows: ‘If I fall in that pit of glowing coals, that’d result in my death or deadly suffering.’” …
> 
> “Suppose a person was to see delightful parks, woods, meadows, and lotus ponds in a dream. But when they woke they couldn’t see them at all. …
> 
> Suppose a man had borrowed some goods—a gentleman’s carriage and fine jewelled earrings— and preceded and surrounded by these he proceeded through the middle of Āpaṇa. When people saw him they’d say: ‘This must be a wealthy man! For that’s how the wealthy enjoy their wealth.’ But when the owners saw him, they’d take back what was theirs. What do you think? Would that be enough for that man to be distressed?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir. Why is that? Because the owners took back what was theirs.” …
> 
> “Suppose there was a dense forest grove not far from a town or village. And there was a tree laden with fruit, yet none of the fruit had fallen to the ground. And along came a man in need of fruit, wandering in search of fruit. Having plunged deep into that forest grove, he would see that tree laden with fruit. He would think: ‘That tree is laden with fruit, yet none of the fruit has fallen to the ground. But I know how to climb a tree. Why don’t I climb the tree, eat as much as I like, then fill my pouch?’ And that’s what he would do. And along would come a second man in need of fruit, wandering in search of fruit, carrying a sharp axe. Having plunged deep into that forest grove, he would see that tree laden with fruit. He would think: ‘That tree is laden with fruit, yet none of the fruit has fallen to the ground. But I don’t know how to climb a tree. Why don’t I chop this tree down at the root, eat as much as I like, then fill my pouch?’ And so he would chop the tree down at the root. What do you think, householder? If the first man, who climbed the tree, doesn’t quickly come down, when that tree fell wouldn’t he break his hand or arm or other limb, resulting in death or deadly suffering for him?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir.”
> 
> “In the same way, a noble disciple reflects: ‘With the simile of the fruit tree the Buddha said that sensuality gives little gratification and much suffering and distress, and the peril there is what’s greater.’ Having seen this as it is with right understanding, they reject equanimity based on multiplicity and develop the equanimity based on unity, where all kinds of grasping at worldly pleasures ceases without remainder.
> 
> Relying on this supreme purity of recollection and equanimity, that noble disciple recollects their many kinds of past lives. That is: one, two, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, a hundred, a thousand, a hundred thousand births; many eons of the world contracting, many eons of the world expanding, many eons of the world contracting and expanding. … They recollect their many kinds of past lives, with features and details.
> 
> Relying on this supreme purity of recollection and equanimity, that noble disciple, with the divine eye that is purified and superhuman, sees beings passing away and reappearing—inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, in a good place or a bad place. … They understand how beings are reborn according to their actions.
> 
> Relying on this supreme purity of recollection and equanimity, that noble disciple abides having entered upon the liberation by mind and liberation by understanding that is devoid of influxes, having for themselves comprehended and realized it right in the present experience.
> 
> That’s how there is the cutting off of business in each and every respect in the noble one’s discipline.
> 
> What do you think, householder? Do you regard yourself as having cut off business in a way comparable to the cutting off of business in each and every respect in the noble one’s discipline?”
> 
> “Who am I compared to one who has cut off business in each and every respect in the noble one’s discipline? I am far from that. Venerable sir, I used to think that the wanderers following other religions were thoroughbreds, and I fed them and treated them accordingly, but they were not actually thoroughbreds. I thought that the bhikkhus were not thoroughbreds, and I fed them and treated them accordingly, but they actually were thoroughbreds. But now I shall understand that the wanderers following other religions are not actually thoroughbreds, and I will feed them and treat them accordingly. And I shall understand that the bhikkhus actually are thoroughbreds, and I will feed them and treat them accordingly. The Buddha has inspired me to have fondness, confidence, and respect for ascetics!
> 
> Excellent, venerable sir! Excellent! As if he were righting the overturned, or revealing the hidden, or pointing out the path to the lost, or lighting a lamp in the dark so people with clear eyes can see what’s there, the Buddha has made the teaching clear in many ways. I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the Dhamma, and to the Saṅgha of bhikkhus. From this day forth, may the Buddha remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”
> 
> Translated by Bhikkhu Anīgha
