<!-- ## Why Is Generosity Good? -->

- [Youtube](https://youtu.be/lTuxOUOPfgk?feature=shared)
- [Suttacentral](https://suttacentral.net/an9.20)

On the most fundamental level, generosity is wholesome because it is founded on non-self-centeredness, or selflessness. Even if a person isn't aware of it, the act of generosity has this principle at its core.

This is different from our common notions of being "selfish" or "unselfish". Acting selfishly or not is secondary to the fundamental level I'm referring to. What I'm talking about is the nature of the act of generosity itself. If you look at it in its own context, it is founded upon an act that goes against self-centeredness. This is why generosity is good, even for people who do it for selfish reasons. A person can be generous for selfish motives, but that still does not cancel out the inherently non-self-centred nature of the act itself. Though their selfishness may have taken possession of that generosity, the act in itself still counts.

Obviously, it's better to not be selfish on any level, but that inherent, non-selfish nature is the sole reason why generosity is good.

So you can see that generosity is good because it contains the fundamental principle of non-self centeredness. But if you can tap into this principle directly, you don't have to be generous.You can go beyond selfishness, even if you don't give anything to anyone for the rest of your life.

But for people who don't reach those levels of mental development and freedom, it's good to practice generosity as a way to emulate that core principle of non-self centredness. Even if they are not fully aware of what they are engaging with, the act itself still counts and still matters.

So, one should be actively engaged in generosity if they have the means, but understand that it is "second best" compared to developing the mind directly. If you can't commit to that more direct practice, or if you're not sure you're doing it right, then focus on generosity. Be generous, be helpful - just in case.

Another thing to add is that an unenlightened person has no choice but to be selfish. Whatever he does, it's because he has a desire to do so. But you can distinguish between a selfishness that perpetuates more selfishness, and a selfishness that can bring more generosity. You want to cultivate the latter.

This is why the Buddha, for example, would actively encourage people by saying, "Think about the benefits of giving because through those acts you'll be born in a heaven." He was telling them to want it and do it. It's not 100% pure, but for the level they are at, it is a good and skillful practice.

## **Aṅguttara Nikāya 9.20**

> ## The Discourse on Velāma
> On one occasion, the Blessed One was staying near Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Park. Then the householder Anāthapiṇḍika went to the Blessed One, bowed, and sat down to one side. The Blessed One then said to him, “Householder, is giving practiced in your family?”
> 
> “Yes, venerable sir,” replied Anāthapiṇḍika, “giving is practiced in my family. But it is only coarse food—sour rice-gruel with just some more sour gruel as a side dish.”
> 
> “Householder,” the Buddha said, “whether one gives a coarse or a fine gift, if one gives it disrespectfully, not thoughtfully, not with one’s own hand, as if throwing it away, and without believing in a future result—then wherever the result of that gift ripens, one’s mind does not incline towards enjoying fine food, fine clothing, or fine vehicles. One’s mind does not incline towards enjoying the five kinds of sensual pleasures. And one’s children, spouse, servants, messengers, and workers do not listen, pay attention, or apply their minds to understand. Why is that? Because this is the result of actions performed disrespectfully.
> 
> But householder, whether one gives a coarse or a fine gift, if one gives it respectfully, thoughtfully, with one’s own hand, not as if throwing it away, and with belief in a future result—then wherever the result of that gift ripens, one’s mind inclines towards enjoying fine food, fine clothing, and fine vehicles. One’s mind inclines towards enjoying the five kinds of sensual pleasures. And one’s children, spouse, servants, messengers, and workers do listen, pay attention, and apply their minds to understand. Why is that? Because this is the result of actions performed respectfully.
> 
> In the past, householder, there was a brahmin named Velāma. He gave a truly great donation. He gave 84,000 golden bowls filled with silver; 84,000 silver bowls filled with gold; 84,000 bronze bowls filled with bullion; 84,000 elephants with golden ornaments, golden banners, and covered with golden nets; 84,000 chariots upholstered with the skins of lions, tigers, and leopards, furnished with yellow woolen blankets, with golden ornaments, golden banners, and covered with golden nets; 84,000 milk-cows with fine linen tethers and bronze milk-pails; 84,000 maidens adorned with jeweled earrings; 84,000 couches spread with long-fleeced woolen blankets, white woolen blankets, and floral-embroidered covers, covered with choice antelope skins, with canopies above and red cushions at both ends; and he gave many thousands of bolts of the finest linen, silk, wool, and cotton. And what can be said of all the food and drink? You would think that rivers of it were flowing.
> 
> Now, householder, you might think: ‘Surely, the brahmin Velāma at that time must have been someone else.’ But you should not see it like that. I myself was the brahmin Velāma at that time. I was the one who gave that great donation. However, householder, at that great giveaway there was no one worthy of the offering, no one to purify that gift.
> 
> Householder, more fruitful than the great donation that the brahmin Velāma gave is to feed a single person who is perfected in view.
> 
> More fruitful than feeding one hundred people perfected in view is to feed a single once-returner.
> 
> More fruitful than feeding one hundred once-returners is to feed a single non-returner.
> 
> More fruitful than feeding one hundred non-returners is to feed a single Arahant.
> 
> More fruitful than feeding one hundred Arahants is to feed a single Paccekabuddha.
> 
> More fruitful than feeding one hundred Paccekabuddhas is to feed a Tathāgata, an Arahant, a Perfectly Self-Enlightened One.
> 
> More fruitful than that is to feed the monastic Sangha headed by the Buddha.
> 
> More fruitful than that is to have a monastery built for the Sangha of the four quarters.
> 
> More fruitful than that is to go for refuge with a confident mind to the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha.
> 
> More fruitful than that is to undertake the training precepts with a confident mind: abstinence from killing living beings, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, and from intoxicants that cause heedlessness. And more fruitful than that is to develop a mind of metta for just the time it takes to milk a cow.
> 
> And householder, more fruitful than the great donation that the brahmin Velāma gave, and more fruitful than feeding one perfected in view, or a hundred of them; or one once-returner, or a hundred of them; or one non-returner, or a hundred of them; or one Arahant, or a hundred of them; or one Paccekabuddha, or a hundred of them; or a Tathāgata; or the Sangha headed by the Buddha; and more fruitful than building a monastery for the Sangha of the four quarters; and more fruitful than taking refuge with a confident heart in the Buddha, Dhamma, and Sangha; and more fruitful than undertaking the five precepts with a confident mind; and more fruitful than developing a mind of metta for the time it takes to milk a cow, is to develop the perception of anicca for just a single moment, the time of a finger-snap.”
