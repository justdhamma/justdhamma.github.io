## Majjhima Nikāya 40 Cūḷaassapurasutta

Thus have I heard—

At one time the Buddha was dwelling among the Aṅgans in a town named Assapura. There, the Buddha addressed the *bhikkhu*: “Venerable sir,” they replied. The Buddha said this:

“People recognize you as ‘*samaṇā*.’ Moreover, when asked who you are, you claim to be ‘*samaṇā*.’ Since you have this designation and make this claim, you should train yourselves like this: ‘We will undertake the proper practice of a *samaṇa*. By doing so, our designation will become true and our claim factual. Consequently, the services of those who provide us with robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicinal requisites will bear great fruit and great benefit for them. And this going forth of ours will not be barren, but fruitful and fertile.’

### The Defilements that Ruin the Practice

“How is a *bhikkhu* not undertaking the proper practice of a *samaṇa*?

“He is not undertaking it if he fails to abandon covetousness when covetous, or ill-will when his *citta* is consumed by ill-will. He fails if he does not abandon anger when angry, hostility when hostile, denigration when denigrating, domineering when domineering, envy when envious, stinginess when stingy, fraudulence when fraudulent, deceit when deceitful, evil desires when having evil desires, and wrong view when holding wrong view.

“Because these stains of a *samaṇa*, faults [blemishes] of a *samaṇa*, and dregs of a *samaṇa*—which lead to states of deprivation and are experienced in bad

**The Inefficacy of Mere Outer Observances**
“I do not say that the sāmañña of a robe-wearer is achieved merely by wearing a robe. Nor do I say that sāmañña is achieved merely by being a naked ascetic, wearing dust and dirt, bathing in water, living at the root of a tree, living in the open air, standing continually, eating at fixed intervals, chanting mantras, or wearing matted hair.

“If the defilements of a robe-wearer were abandoned merely by wearing a robe, his friends, companions, and relatives would have made him wear a robe from birth! Because these stains, faults, and dregs of a samaṇa—which lead to states of deprivation—are abandoned, I say he is undertaking the proper practice of a samaṇa.

# The Right Kind of Questioning

In the above discourse, the Buddha addresses the nature of the *brahmacariya* (The noble lifestyle). He tells the monks that since they claim to be samaṇās, they must practice the way proper to a samaṇā so that their designations may be true and their claims genuine.

How does one fail to practice this way? The Buddha explains that as long as a person has a citta filled with covetousness, ill will, defilements, and wrong views - and has not abandoned them - they are not practicing the proper way. He makes it clear that liberation does not come about through asceticism or the mere wearing of rag robes. If freedom were a matter of wearing the right clothing, your relatives could dress you in them at birth and the work would be done.

The right practice occurs when someone who has a defilement or wrong view actually abandons it. He sees himself purified of evil, unwholesome states and liberated from them. When he sees this directly, gladness is born in him. When he is glad, happiness is born, and his body becomes calmed.

## Reading and Realization

When you read that passage, it sounds obvious and self-explanatory. Yet, nobody seems to actually do it. Even people committed to Buddhist practice fail to directly see the abandonment of these states and the resulting gladness. Instead, their practice is based on a sort of blind faith - an approximation of, "I'll do this random method, and then this result might happen."

Direct knowledge is completely absent. Direct knowledge means the present, live streamed recognition of: "*This is wholesome, this is unwholesome; the unwholesome is abandoned, and because of that I am glad and happy.*"

Not many people recognize that this direct recognition is *all* there is to the practice. All the work that needs to be done is contained within that single realization. Not elsewhere, not before, and not after. You understand the unwholesome, you abandon it, and then you know there are no unwholesome states present. Gladness arises automatically because you have done the right work.

The only natural conclusion for why people aren't seeing this - and are instead distracted by reliance on all sorts of other techniques, faiths, and traditions - is because they simply don't understand what wholesome and unwholesome actually are.

If you ask someone if they know what ill will is, they will confidently say yes. But that is the core of the problem. A *puthujjana* (an ordinary, uninstructed person) - must

first recognize that he is a puthujjana. That means his working definitions of ill will, non-ill will, sensuality, right view, and wrong view are flawed.

Nobody denies that a beginner doesn't fully know. But even if you have been practicing for decades, if you are not yet a sotāpanna, you must not regard your own knowledge as valid. With that humility, you keep making an effort until you verify the truth with your own direct understanding of freedom from suffering.

You must frequently question yourself: "I read these suttas about ill will, and I tell myself what I should do, but do I even know what the phenomenon of ill will actually is? What am I designating when I say 'ill will'? Am I referring to a state of mind?"

Do not look for outside information to answer this. Ask yourself what you are pointing at in your very experience. Is it a pressure in your chest? Is it the redness in your face? If you look closely, you realize those are merely the physical results of the ill will. So what is the ill will itself?

People mistakenly assume a puthujjana can become a sotāpanna by merely upgrading their understanding - by pasting over new truths onto what they already know about life. That is completely the wrong direction. You can only become a sotāpanna when you completely undo everything that makes you a puthujjana. You do not build new views on top of your current state. Right view only takes place when the foundation of the puthujjana has been dismantled.

**The Method of Questioning**

You can simply ask, "Am I engaging in ill will now?" You might answer yes or no, but you must press further: "Do I actually understand what ill will is? What am I even asking myself?"

It is this lack of right reflection on basic designations that keeps the puthujjana trapped. He maintains wrong views because he hasn't uprooted his assumptions at their foundation. No matter how many stories you build, if the foundation is flawed, the right structure cannot stand.

Instead of trying to find answers, you must understand your questions. Take any of the five aggregates - feeling, for instance. When you say, "I'm feeling like this today," don't worry about the specific content of the emotion. Look at the designation of the word "feeling" itself. Ask yourself: "What am I referring to? What am I designating with this word? Can I discern its actual nature?"

Your theoretical, third-person knowledge is an assumption used to avoid direct insight. When you say, 'My feeling is such and such,' you must see what you are actually pointing at without the shield of abstract theory.

This is why, in the suttas, monks trying to understand a phenomenon would say it out loud: "Feeling, feeling, what is feeling?" Say it out loud to bring it to your mind. Then, within that enduring experience, start discerning where that feeling actually resides.

You will quickly realize that your understanding of the aggregates is completely muddled. People use words like "matter," "feeling," "perception," and "consciousness," but their understanding is just a concoction of self-contradictory concepts. And on top of that confusion, they try to practice the Dhamma by adding more concepts! But the right practice of Dhamma is undoing ignorance, not adding right knowledge on top of it.

You undo ignorance by putting your basic experience into question. Don't deny it, don't affirm it; just question it to see what is presently enduring. Jumping to a third-person view - and saying, "There is no feeling, everything is impermanent" - is just serving up another assumption.

Put the theories aside. Ask: "Feeling, feeling, what is my feeling right here, right now?" If the answer is, "I'm feeling pleasant," look at the feeling aspect of that pleasantness. Even if your understanding is muddled, you are still referring to something. That "something" is your starting point.

**The Root of the Unwholesome**

To question yourself rightly implies you are willing to look at what you are experiencing and see if it actually matches your theories. This is how you peel back the layers of muddled thinking. You don't need to know all the answers before you begin. You start questioning, and that is how you realize how much you don't know. Recognizing your own ignorance tells you exactly what you need to learn.

Let us look closely at ill will. How would you describe it experientially? First and most obvious: it is unpleasant. It is a resistance in regard to a feeling.

You must be careful not to slip into psychologizing here, because that is just assuming a theoretical, third-person view of your own experience. Keep dwelling directly on the phenomenon without fabricating explanations. Nor should you mystify it by calling it "inexplicable." (Calling it inexplicable is assigning it a property, which is a contradiction.) The criteria are simple: don't contradict yourself, and don't assume a third-person view.

If ill will is essentially unpleasant, what is the nature of your resistance? What is it you don't want? Is the unpleasantness of the ill will that you are rejecting?...

Anger is an indulgence used to mask the underlying pain of a feeling you cannot control. The act of retaliation is an attempt to escape the displeasure of the presently

arisen pressure. To undo that anger, you must stop enjoying the indulgence and see the anger for what it truly is: inherently painful.

If you are able to patiently endure the unpleasant feeling for as long as it remains, you won't give in to ill will.

What motivates ill will? Running away from your own displeasure. If you want to cut off ill will, stop running away from the displeasure. Without that escape route, anger has no outlet.

This applies equally to envy, sensuality, and deceitfulness. If you take an unpleasant feeling for granted, you will take your habitual reaction to it for granted, and unwholesome states will inevitably proliferate. If you run around trying to chase down every individual unwholesome thought to trim it back, you will exhaust yourself and fail. If you want that defilement to end, you don't trim the branches; you kill it at the root. Uproot your attitude toward the presently arisen feeling, and the pathways for unwholesome action dry up completely.

**The Inverted Practice**

Modern practice has turned the Buddha's teachings completely upside down. People read that a monk abandons the wrong view, gains the right view, is purified, and as a result, abides pervading the world with a mind of metta. But many people invert this. They assume: "I will actively try to pervade metta, and then I will abandon ill will and gain samadhi."

They try to use visualization or wishful thinking to cure their defilements because the ignorant mind is addicted to doing things. When you tell a puthujjana that they need to understand the nature of their actions, their immediate, panicky response is: "Okay, so what do I DO?"

That is the threshold of their mind. They cannot think below the level of action. You tell them to understand the unwholesome and abandon the wrong view, and they ask for a physical or mental technique to achieve it. So they sit in a room, send positive thoughts to their enemies, and hope that through this magical doing, they will become free from the need to do. They remain completely oblivious to the contradiction of trying to free oneself from action by engaging in more action.

Metta is a result of right understanding, not the method to achieve it. The practice is not about what you do; it is about whether you understand the phenomenon of doing.

In the case of ill will, you already know what you should not do. You must not engage with it at face value. You must abstain from unwholesome action.

Once you establish that restraint, you can investigate. If you are currently restraining ill will, don't theorize about why you are angry. Allow the presently arisen ill will to endure and ask: "What are the characteristics of this experience as a whole?"

As we established, ill will is always an unpleasant pressure. The actions you take on account of ill will are actually rooted in your attitude toward that pressure. If a feeling applies pressure to you and you cannot help but respond, that feeling controls you. Even if you say you want to be free, the fact that you keep responding to the pressure proves your absolute subjection.

To become free, you must first stop responding to the pressure. Restraint is not a moral duty; it is the laboratory condition required to witness your own subjection. Once restrained, clarity begins. You see that restraint doesn't magically cure the ill will, but you understand exactly why it is necessary: *I have to stop giving in to the pressure in order to free myself from the pressure*. You see this not as a theory, but as a presently enduring reality.

**Just There**

Once restrained, you realize you have no say over which aspect of the ill will bothers you. It arises as a whole package. Ill will inherently presses you.

At this point, your concern must shift. You are no longer concerned with the narrative of the ill will. You are entirely concerned with your attitude of craving - your gratuitous desire for the disagreeable feeling to go away.

You apply the questioning again: "Feeling, feeling. What is this feeling?" Look at the disagreeable feeling underlying the ill will right here, right now. How would you describe its nature?

If you honestly assess an enduring, background feeling, the only true answer is: "It is just there."

If something is "just there" - if you have no idea why it arose, how long it will stay, or how to make it go away - why are you regarding that thing as yours? Why do you take your reaction to it so seriously? Your reaction implies you can control this thing that is oppressing you. But looking closely, you realize you are entirely subjected to it.

If feeling were truly yours, you would only ever experience the feelings you want. The fact that you constantly experience feelings you don't want proves you are not in control. That is the nature of sensuality. It is entirely rooted in craving in response to an arisen feeling.

Craving is not a process in time. It is a simultaneously present attitude toward a simultaneously present feeling. Whether the state is envy, ill will, or greed, it all boils down to how that experience is being felt overall. Are you trying to indulge it, get rid

of it, or distract yourself from it? Craving is what resisting, welcoming, and covering up all have in common: it is the desire to replace what is currently there.

Even toward neutral feeling, the Buddha noted a tendency toward ignorance, distraction, and oblivion. That tendency is where the craving hides. You want to override the present feeling, but how futile is it to try and override something when you have no say in its existence? You only entertain the notion of control because you don't truly understand the nature of what you are resisting. Ignorance fuels the craving.

**Ignorance and Insight**

If you stop ignoring the reality of the feeling, there is simply no room for craving. You wouldn't even have to *try* to disengage.

This is what right mindfulness is. It is not a vague awareness of everything and nothing. It is mindfulness of the presently enduring feeling in the background of your experience. What is your present mood? Is it pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral? That is your starting point. If you do not lose sight of the general mood, you cannot ignore its nature.

The nature of these arisen things is *anicca* and *dukkha*. The attitudes of resistance and craving stem entirely from the delusion: "I am in charge. I am in control." But if you press yourself, you realize: "The feeling is just there. It oppresses me. I do not oppress it. I am subjected to it." Even the most self-centered megalomaniac cannot ignore the fact that their sense of self and their reactions are entirely secondary to a feeling that has already arisen on its own.

Seeing this subjection is seeing *anicca*. As the Buddha said: *Sabbe sankhārā aniccā, Sabbe sankhārā dukkhā, Sabbe dhammā anattā*.

You must see that the presently arisen things determining your experience have arisen on their own and are oppressing you. *That is what anicca and dukkha are. Dukkha is not just physical pain; it is the unease of realizing you are fundamentally not in control.*

When you practice the proper *vipassanā*, the way I have just described, you look past the pleasantness or unpleasantness of a mood and look at its universal characteristics. You don't sit there chanting, "It's anicca." You ask: "What is anicca for me right now?"

You see the feeling is "just there." You are not its creator. It cannot be owned. You have no choice in the matter. Are your feelings subordinate to you, or is your volition subordinate to the feeling that is presently oppressing you?

This investigation must be done on the level of the most general background mood, because any lesser, passing feeling can be easily appropriated by your conceit to maintain the illusion of control. When you realize that your entire experience is anicca and oppressing you (dukkha), the delusion that you are the master controller shatters. That shattering is the experience of anatta.

That realization undermines your very sense of self. From then on, you simply stay mindful of the aggregates arising and passing away. By staying mindful, you cannot ignore their nature. By not ignoring their nature, you cannot proliferate craving. And without craving, you cannot suffer.
